Initial Yarn

8 years into my career in the Royal New Zealand Infantry Regiment I find myself standing in the guard hut at ISAF HQ in the "Green Zone" of Kabul, Afghanistan. We had been on the ground less than two hours and already we had navigated through the city despite the Improvised Explosive Device (IED) threats we were warned of moments after we landed. Now, in this cramped little hut, three of us who had just landed were standing behind our team leader while he tries to explain the details of our arrival to the Italian Military Police (MP) Officer in charge of security. The MP feels threatened and reaches for his pistol...I reach for mine. Is this really how my first visit to Afghanistan is supposed to start? I'm supposed to kill this Italian MP so that he doesn't kill my friends? Am I really going to start an international incident? If it means my friends are safe then yes...

Kia ora, I'm Phill Keelan and after 19 years in the Royal New Zealand Infantry Regiment it's time for a change of pace and a change in career. Luckily, no one was hurt in the guard hut that day. But the MP did feel a bit embarrassed from that day forward. This was not the first sticky situation in my career and it sure as hell wasn't the last. Such is the nature of being in the infantry.

Ethical Decisions

There are still some people who do not like those of us within the infantry. They think that we are war mongers. I've even been called a "baby killer" to my face. But we don't get to pick where our government sends us. We can only choose to go or not. And we go to protect our friends who also go. The question I would ask others who query why I would choose to go is "if not me then who?". I'm good at what I do and believe I have the skills and knowledge to ensure I come home. But if I don't go then someone else has to. Someone else's son/daughter, brother/sister, father/mother. Will they fair better than I would? Can I bear to subject their families to that loss should the worse happens? And so I go...

This paired with a strict and somewhat violent upbringing made joining the infantry seem like natural progression. Yet with all the violence I saw throughout the world and all the pain and suffering it caused there was a shift in my mindset. The drive to enact violence had been put in a dormant state and a sense of "manaakitanga ki tatou rangatira" had been brought to the forefront. And so my values changed. So too did the journey.

Strengths

For personal development reasons I have conducted the Myer's Briggs Personality Test several times throughout the years and always end up as the Logician. The Logician's strengths include being analytical and curious. These two traits have stuck with me through my career and have helped thrive through continuously learning and easily developing new skills. These will help me learn software development through the analysis of what is being taught, creating problems through being curious, then problem solving through analytical thinking.

Pitfalls

Unfortunately the Logician comes with its pitfalls as every personality does. As a logician empathy is not a strength of mine. This was also confirmed through a Hogan's test I done as part of some leadership development I conducted. Because I was able to identify this flaw through the Hogan's test I was able to create prompts to ensure the I am more empathetic to my friends and colleagues so I feel this is no longer a problem. However, the analytical trait can diminish response times and so the conversation sometimes moves on before an ideal answer comes to mind. Because of this I may struggle to keep up in a structure learning environment but this will not be a problem in a flipped classroom.

Working With Others

Being in the infantry isn't always the excitement of near death experiences. Ninety percent of the time it's boring and monotonous. Like with every job you're not going to get along or see eye to eye with everyone. So being able to communicate effectively becomes a big aspect of the job. One of the last positions I was in was the Sniper Supervisor for the 2nd/1st Battalion in Burnham Military Camp. My role was to be the centre of all sniper matters within the battalion. Because of the qualifications required for the position no one else could do my job and so no one could tell me how to do my job. However, snipers are the only thing in an infantry battalion and so there is always resistance and tension from other capabilities. Despite these tensions the capabilities nee to learn how to support each other. One is nothing without the other and compromise is key. In saying that, sometimes "No" is acceptable.

One example of how I dealt with resistance when working with others was when a senior member (two ranks higher than me) was trying to tell me what my snipers were doing during an attack (exercise). I explained to him that that's not in their job description but he was persistent that he does not care and the snipers were going to do it anyway because the Commanding Officer needs every aspect that he explained. I identified an alternate way which meant I had to do more work but my snipers could focus on doing what they're trained to and, reluctantly, he was happy with that. I then gritted my teeth, walked away, and actioned what I said it would. Even now this I can see that this was the best of the worst outcomes. The benefit being that my lads weren't used ineffectively and so they were able to maintain their job satisfaction. The trade off of creating more work for myself in that moment was appreciated by the snipers in turn making my life easier later on down the track.
